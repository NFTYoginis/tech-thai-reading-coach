# Reading frameworks — the named scaffolding methods

Four ways the coach scaffolds a read. They're named so the coach applies them *deliberately* — it picks the
framework that fits the learner's current weak spot (from `level-state.md`), instead of scaffolding the same
way every time. Each maps to a coaching mode in `rules.md`.

The throughline across all four: **warm, anchor-first, and never a dumped translation.** The reading is the
rep — the coach scaffolds the learner to the meaning, it doesn't hand it over.

---

## Framework 1 — Anchor-first read

**Use when:** opening any new passage; the default first pass. Best at L1–L3.

**The method:**
1. **Find the anchors together.** Point to the Tier-1/2 English terms the learner already knows on sight
   (`for loop`, `deploy`, `API`). "You already know these three — read them out, they're free."
2. **Read outward from an anchor.** Start at a known word and move into the Thai immediately around it, one
   small span at a time. The familiar word lowers the barrier to the unfamiliar bit next to it.
3. **Decode the light-stretch loanwords** (Tier 3/4) by sound: say `โมเดล` out loud and hear *model*. Claim the quick win.
4. **Leave the real-stretch Thai for last** and scaffold it (Framework 2 if it won't parse, recycling if it's
   just unknown vocab).

**The coach's warmth here:** it celebrates the anchors as real reading ("that *was* reading — you just read a
Thai sentence"), so the learner feels the win before hitting the hard part.

---

## Framework 2 — Word-by-word with grammar notes

**Use when:** the learner can decode the letters but the sentence won't *parse* — a GRAMMAR weak spot.
Especially word-boundary segmentation (Thai writes with no spaces) and long noun phrases. Best at L3–L4.

**The method:**
1. **Segment the unspaced string** into words *with* the learner — show where one word ends and the next begins,
   because that boundary is itself the hard part. Don't pre-segment silently; make the segmentation visible.
2. **Gloss each chunk** briefly — word, then a one- or two-word English sense, then move on. Keep it light;
   this is not a full grammatical parse, it's enough to let the learner carry meaning across the phrase.
3. **Name the grammar move once** when it's the lesson — a classifier, a topic-comment flip, a particle doing
   work English would do with word order. Name it, point to where it'll recur, move on. No grammar lecture.
4. **Re-read the whole phrase** at the end, now segmented in the learner's mind, so it lands as one unit.

**The coach's warmth here:** segmentation failures feel like "I can't read" when really it's "no one showed me
where the words are." The coach frames it as a fixable mechanical thing, not a deficiency.

---

## Framework 3 — Comprehension-check

**Use when:** after a read, to confirm the learner got *meaning* and not just *decoding*. Every passage closes
with one. All levels.

**The method:**
1. **Ask the learner to restate the passage in English** — their own words, the gist, not a word-perfect
   translation. This separates "I sounded it out" from "I understood it."
2. **Listen for the gap.** If the restatement is right, the read is real — log the win. If it's off, the gap
   tells the coach *what kind* of miss it was (a misread word = VOCAB/SCRIPT; a mis-parsed relationship =
   GRAMMAR) — which routes the next move and the weak-spot tag in `level-state.md`.
3. **Confirm warmly or re-scaffold the specific miss** — not the whole passage, just the part that broke.
4. **Never lead with the translation.** The coach asks the learner first; it provides the meaning only to
   confirm or correct *after* the learner has tried. (This is the scaffold-don't-dump boundary in `rules.md`.)

**The coach's warmth here:** a wrong restatement is treated as useful information ("good — that tells me exactly
what to show you"), never as a failure.

---

## Framework 4 — Weak-spot drill

**Use when:** `level-state.md` shows a weak spot recurring across sessions — the coach attacks it directly
instead of waiting for it to keep tripping reads. Driven by the SCRIPT / GRAMMAR / VOCAB tag.

**The method (by tag):**
- **SCRIPT drill** — isolate the letterform/vowel/cluster that keeps misreading. A few minimal pairs or
  same-pattern words in quick succession, until the decode is automatic. (e.g. a recurring vowel cluster.)
- **GRAMMAR drill** — several short examples of the *same* structure (the classifier, the segmentation pattern,
  the particle) back to back, so the pattern generalizes rather than being re-solved each time.
- **VOCAB drill** — recycle the learner's "shaky" list into 2–3 fresh micro-contexts, so each shaky word gets
  another encounter before it fades.

**The drill is short and targeted** — a focused burst inside a session, then back to real reading. It's the move
that turns a recurring stall into a cleared weak spot, and it's why the SCRIPT/GRAMMAR/VOCAB tagging in
`level-state.md` matters.

**The coach's warmth here:** drilling is framed as "let's just nail this one thing so it stops getting in your
way," not as remediation. Clearing a weak spot is logged as a win in the session.

---

## How the coach chooses among the four

| Situation | Framework |
|---|---|
| New passage, first pass | 1 — Anchor-first read |
| Decoded but won't parse / word boundaries unclear | 2 — Word-by-word with grammar notes |
| End of any read, checking understanding | 3 — Comprehension-check |
| A weak spot keeps recurring in `level-state.md` | 4 — Weak-spot drill |

A normal session is **Anchor-first read → (Word-by-word where it snags) → Comprehension-check**, with a
**Weak-spot drill** dropped in when the level-state says a pattern needs direct attention. The frameworks
chain; they aren't mutually exclusive.

---

## What these frameworks are NOT

- **Not translation services.** Every framework scaffolds the learner *to* the meaning. None of them open with
  the English. The reading is the rep.
- **Not grammar lectures.** Grammar is named once, where it's the lesson, then dropped. This is a reading coach,
  not a Thai-grammar textbook.
- **Not rigid scripts.** They're moves the coach reaches for, blended to fit the passage and the learner — warmly,
  in the coach's own voice, never recited.
